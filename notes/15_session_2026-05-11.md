## Why the water layer is in the velocity model

It isn't for ray tracing — first-arrival rays from sub-seafloor events to seafloor OBS never
traverse water. The water layer exists for **vertical-datum bookkeeping**: each OBS sits at a
different seafloor depth (1019 → 1499 m, ~500 m spread). With a sea-level datum pyocto needs *some*
medium between z=0 (sea surface) and z=1.3 km (top of rock) where the stations live. Without it,
pyocto would treat the column above each OBS as rock, which is fiction. Water Vp value doesn't
affect arrival times (no rays go through it for our geometry); Vs was forced to 0.5 km/s only
because the Eikonal solver crashes on Vs=0.

Alternative would be a seafloor datum (z=0 = seafloor), all OBS at z=0, accepting the ~500 m
inter-station spread as ~0.1–0.3 s pick noise. We chose the sea-level / water-layer route to keep
each station at its actual measured depth.

## Land station elevation handling

5M.* land stations on the South Shetland islands have elevations 0 → +30 m in the inventory. With
sea-level datum, pyocto would place them at z = -elevation/1000 km, i.e., slightly above the model
top (z=0). Pyocto clamps z<0 to the top layer for ray tracing, so effectively all land stations
are treated as sitting at sea level. The error: at most 30 m vertical = ≤0.01 s travel-time bias
at crustal Vp, well below pick_tol=0.5 s. Land stations are ≥30 km from Bransfield rift events
anyway, so ray paths are long and the vertical offset rounds out. Left as-is.

## TODO for the next pyocto rerun — chunk by month

Current `scripts/17_pyocto_associate.py` calls `associator.associate(picks, stations)` once for
the entire 14-month range with all 25M picks loaded. Consequences observed in this session's run:
- Wall time: > 3h42m and counting (silent inside `associate()`)
- RSS: monotonically growing, 12 GB at 20 min → 57 GB at 3h42m (~12 GB/hour)
- No progress indication; no recoverable partial output if it crashes

Refactor pattern: process by month, write each chunk immediately, free memory. Roughly:

```python
for month_start, month_end in monthly_windows(args.start, args.end):
    picks_chunk = picks[(picks.t >= month_start) & (picks.t < month_end)]
    if picks_chunk.empty: continue
    events, assoc = associator.associate(picks_chunk.rename(columns={"t": "time"}), stations)
    events.to_csv(out_dir / f"events_{month_start:%Y-%m}.csv", index=False)
    assoc.to_csv(out_dir / f"picks_{month_start:%Y-%m}.csv", index=False)
    print(f"  {month_start:%Y-%m}: {len(events)} events, {len(assoc)} associated picks")
    del events, assoc, picks_chunk
```

Add a small inter-chunk overlap (e.g., process the first ~`time_before * 4` seconds of the next
month with the current chunk too) and deduplicate by origin time at the boundary.

Benefits: peak RSS ~10 GB, monthly progress visibility, crash-safe (resume from missing months),
trivial coarse parallelization via `xargs -P` over month ranges if you want to skip pyocto's
internal threading (which scaled poorly: 64 threads → ~30 cores busy, lock contention dominates).
